Q3 — Improvement Roadmap

Five weeks, ten thousand dollars, two tools. I'd put three of the five weeks into Jatayu and two into Outreach. The Jatayu side is more than half the grade once Q1b is folded in, and its single biggest weakness — there's no measurement loop, so every claim about filter precision is a guess — caps the impact of every other improvement on this page. Fix that first and everything else gets sharper.

One note up front: the impact numbers below are honest estimates from building the tool, not measured baselines. The whole point of Jatayu's first improvement is to convert them into measured baselines. Until that lands, treat them as the shape of the bet, not the answer.

---

## Tool 1 — Jatayu (USD 5,000, 15 working days)

### 1. A labelled eval set with a regression harness — $1,500, 5 days

Today there's no automated way to tell whether a config change helps or hurts. When I tighten an exclusion or rebalance a sub-score weight, I run a calibration pull, eyeball eight profiles, and decide it looks better. That's not a measurement, that's a vibe.

The fix is a 250-profile labelled set — about thirty-five hours for a domain-aware contractor at twenty bucks an hour, so roughly $700 — that gives every profile a ground-truth tier (disqualified / core / adjacent / weak) and a within-tier rank. Coresignal credits to assemble a representative seed pool from past mandates run about $500. CI minutes another $300. Then a `jatayu eval` CLI command recomputes filter precision (90%-plus fits in the raw pull) and a Kendall tau rank correlation on every config change, wired into a git pre-commit hook on `configs/*.yaml` so a bad edit can't ship.

**Expected impact:** converts the *estimated* ~45% filter precision into a tracked metric with a target band of ≥70%; cuts the config iteration cycle from half a day of eyeballing to under five minutes per change; catches the regression class where a "helpful" exclusion silently tanks recall by five points or more. The meta-impact matters most: the two improvements below become falsifiable instead of aspirational.

**Trade-off:** a week with no user-visible feature. Hard to justify in a vacuum, justified here because everything else on this page is unfalsifiable without it.

### 2. Scope-signal extraction on the candidate Profile — $1,800, 5 days

The current `Profile` object (jatayu/scoring/profile.py:27-37) carries title, company, duration, and firm size. It does not carry anything that distinguishes *sole-officer scope* from *one-of-five-officers scope*. The scorer has to infer ownership from headcount alone, and that ties near-equal candidates. A 50-person firm with a one-person compliance team and a 50-person firm with a four-person compliance team look identical to the LLM, even though the role shape is completely different.

Coresignal's Clean API already returns `management_level`, `direct_reports`, `is_working`, and `company_type` — none of these are surfaced today. Surfacing them and rewriting the sub-score prompts to read them explicitly, rather than nudging the model to "infer from firm size," is the work. Five days between engineering time and prompt iteration runs against the eval set from #1. Budget breaks roughly into $1,200 engineering / Anthropic billing for the prompt rewrite, and $600 LLM tokens for prompt-tuning runs against the eval set.

**Expected impact:** measured against the gold rank order from #1, top-10 Kendall tau should move from somewhere around 0.45 to somewhere around 0.65 by breaking near-ties where one candidate has documented sole ownership and another doesn't. The bigger win is generalization — portfolio breadth is the analogous scope signal for Mandate B's Investment Director role, and the same extraction pipeline serves both.

**Trade-off:** the per-profile prompt gets about 30% wider, so token cost per scored candidate goes from roughly two-tenths of a cent to three-tenths. Negligible at 300 profiles, but a real line item if we ever push to ten thousand.

### 3. Tighter firm classifier and a pre-collect sparseness gate — $1,700, 5 days

Two cheap precision fixes that share a debug session.

The classifier today uses substring matching for industries (`firm_classifier._industry_hit`, sourcing/firm_classifier.py:55-73), which means anything containing "Capital" or "Management" can classify as boutique AM. This is how a Rio Tinto compliance officer ended up in our calibration pull. Switching to `match_phrase` semantics and requiring at least one non-null attribute closes the hole.

Separately, we currently spend a Coresignal collect credit on profiles too thin to score — two experiences, no duration, no skills, no headline — they come back as low-confidence ~45s and get discarded. A scorability probe on the cheap preview endpoint *before* the collect call would skip them. About $1,500 of engineering plus $200 in preview-endpoint probes for experimentation (preview is one credit per search regardless of profile count, so this is mostly trial-and-error budget).

**Expected impact:** against the eval set, six to ten points of filter precision recovered from the substring fix, and fifteen to twenty percent fewer collect credits burned on un-scorable profiles. Both measurable against #1.

**Trade-off:** the sparseness gate slightly hurts recall on senior candidates with deliberately thin profiles — exactly the Mandate B exposure. Accept it because the scorer can't generate signal from a near-empty record anyway, and the credit saved buys deeper coverage in pools that *do* score well.

## Tool 2 — Outreach (USD 5,000, 10 working days)

### 1. An LLM-as-judge grounding validator — $1,800, 4 days

The current grounding validator (jatayu/outreach/generator.py:177-193) is a token-overlap check: at least one word from the claim's `grounded_in` string has to appear somewhere in the known-facts blob. This is too generous. "Headed a 40-person team" passes as grounded in "Head of Compliance" because the word "head" appears in both. At a hundred recipients, even a five-percent pass-through rate means five completely fabricated specifics getting signed off as verified. That's exactly the failure mode the brief watches for.

Replace it with a Haiku call: hand the model the claim, the claimed grounding fact, and the exhaustive known-facts list, and have it return *entailed / partial / not_entailed* per claim. Drafts with any not-entailed claim go back for regeneration with the failing claim stripped from the allowed-fact set. Four days, mostly engineering and iteration on a 50-claim adversarial test set. The judge tokens themselves are negligible at Haiku rates — a 100-recipient run is under fifty cents — so $500 of the budget covers iteration and the other $1,300 is engineering time.

**Expected impact:** hallucination pass-through on the adversarial set from roughly 15% to under 3%. Eliminates the class of plausible-but-invented specifics that token-overlap currently waves through.

**Trade-off:** about two seconds of added latency per recipient. Fine for a generate-then-review tool. The alternative — a stricter hand-authored rule-based validator — would over-reject true grounding and need constant maintenance as the prompt evolves.

### 2. Parallel runner with checkpointing and a cross-recipient repetition guard — $1,500, 3 days

Two structural scale fixes that share a refactor.

The current runner (jatayu/outreach/runner.py:44-64) is a sequential for-loop with no exception handling. At a hundred recipients that's past five minutes of wall-clock, and one rate-limit error midway through means starting from zero. Moving to a thread pool with per-recipient checkpoint writes — resume from the last successful index — and a configurable concurrency cap (default 8) brings the same run under a minute and makes a mid-batch failure cost nothing.

The second piece: drafts are generated in isolation today, with no awareness of what's been written for other recipients in the same batch. Two recipients at similar firms can end up with near-identical opening hooks, which defeats the entire premise of the tool. After all drafts generate, run an embeddings pass over each draft's opening sentence (`text-embedding-3-small`, cheap), and any draft with cosine similarity above 0.85 against another in the batch gets flagged for regeneration with an "avoid repeating this opening pattern" instruction.

$1,300 engineering plus $200 in embeddings and regeneration tokens for a 100-recipient batch.

**Expected impact:** wall-clock on 100 recipients from about five minutes to under a minute; max opening-pair similarity held below 0.85 (vs roughly 0.92 today when archetypes overlap); clean resumption from any mid-run failure.

**Trade-off:** more parallelism puts more pressure on Anthropic's rate limits. Cap concurrency at eight and add Retry-After-aware backoff rather than pushing wider.

### 3. Sparse-tier public-data connector and itemized uncertainty UX — $1,700, 3 days

The richness tier in `enrich.py` (lines 54-67) is the right idea, but the thresholds are hardcoded numbers picked without data, and the operator only sees a binary `review_required` flag. They can't tell *what* was missing or *what specifically* the draft is hedging on.

Two fixes. First, a `PublicSignals` connector that lifts a name-only recipient to having at least firm-level grounding — Companies House (UK, free), ACRA (SG, free but needs an entity-resolution build), and NewsAPI (about $450 a year, prorated). A name plus a firm becomes a name plus a firm plus a recent funding round, plus a leadership-change press release, plus whatever else surfaces. About $700 of the budget goes to API subscriptions and the remaining $1,000 to engineering time on the connector and the UX surfacing.

Second, expose `missing_fields` and `low_confidence_claims` arrays on every draft so the operator opens it and immediately sees which gaps drove the hedging. Review time per sparse draft drops from three minutes of re-reading-to-guess to thirty seconds of reading-a-flagged-list.

**Expected impact:** sparse-tier recipients picking up at least one grounded personalization hook from roughly 0% today to about 55%. Operator sparse-review time cut by roughly 5x.

**Trade-off:** spends 34% of the Outreach budget on the hardest two of five recipients. Rich recipients see no direct lift from this. That's the right call because the brief explicitly marks down picking only easy recipients — the question is whether you can handle the thin ones gracefully, and the answer should be yes.

## The cross-tool trade-off

Three weeks to Jatayu and two to Outreach means Outreach ships without CRM or send-side integration — no suppression list, no reply tracking, no scheduled send. The brief is explicit that send-side work isn't required and that token cost isn't graded, so the tool stays *generate-and-audit, human-sends*. The alternative — spend three of the five weeks on Outreach send infrastructure — would optimize the lower-weighted tool while leaving the higher-weighted tool's impact claims unfalsifiable. I'd rather have one tool I can prove works than two tools I can demo well.
