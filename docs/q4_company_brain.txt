# Q4 — Company Brain for an Executive Search Business

## 1. What it means here, and what it doesn't

If you Google "Company Brain" you get the obvious version: a RAG layer over Slack, Notion, customer calls, support tickets, so anyone in the company can ask "how does the X integration work" and get the right answer. That's a fine product for a software business where the bottleneck is institutional knowledge buried in chat logs.

It's the wrong frame for executive search.

A search firm doesn't compete on access to information. Every competitor in Singapore has the same Coresignal seat, the same LinkedIn search bar, can read the same press releases. What a search firm actually sells is a judgment call about who a specific client will hire, made under that client's particular taste — which often diverges from what they wrote in the brief. The candidate database is commodity. The judgment is not.

So in this context the Company Brain is the system that captures *decisions and their consequences* and compounds them into reusable fit judgment. Not "what do we know," but "who did we put in front of whom, what happened, and why." The candidate profiles are the rows; the labels — advanced, rejected, declined, placed, retained at six months, retained at twelve — are what no vendor can sell you.

## 2. Architecture

Three coupled stores, plus a derived model on top.

An **entity graph** that joins people, firms, clients, mandates, and recruiters into a single web. An **outcome event log** that records every decision moment — shortlisted, advanced, rejected with reason, declined, placed, churned — with a timestamp and an author. A **vector index** over briefs, debrief notes, and outreach replies for semantic recall. And on top of those, a **per-client preference model** trained on the event log that learns each client's revealed taste from their advance/reject pattern, separate from what they wrote in any brief.

What this looks like in use: a recruiter is given a new mandate — "Head of Compliance at an SG asset manager." She opens the mandate console and the Brain shows her the three closest mandates we've ever run, who was shortlisted on each, who advanced, who actually placed and is still there nine months on. It surfaces the feeder firms that have produced placed candidates for this role shape, not just role title. It shows candidates we previously approached for similar mandates, their outcome, and how warm the relationship is now. And then, the part competitors can't replicate, it pre-ranks the new longlist by *this specific client's revealed preferences*, if we've worked with them before, rather than by the generic brief alone.

The other half of the architecture is the part most write-ups skip, and it's the one that actually matters for us. **The Brain isn't fed by a separate logging discipline. It's fed by Jatayu's workflow itself.**

Jatayu is a ten-stage pipeline where each stage is a deliberate human checkpoint sitting on top of LLM work. The strategy generator drafts the brief into a structured search plan; the recruiter edits the market map, the targeting filters, the off-limits list. A pre-flight pass audits the filter for known data-quality patterns before any credit is spent. Calibrate pulls eight benchmark candidates and asks for a thumbs up or thumbs down on each. The longlist is triaged candidate-by-candidate with accept, park, or reject. Individual sub-scores and hard gates can be overridden with a note that gets stored. Status flows through contacted, responded, screened, client review, interview, offer, with each transition timestamped. The architecture is "LLM proposes, human disposes" at every gate, on purpose — not because we don't trust the model, but because every override is a *label* the firm needs to keep, and the only way to keep labels reliably is to make producing them a byproduct of doing the work.

A Brain that ingests documents that already exist captures whatever discipline a firm happened to have. A Brain wired into a workflow like ours captures the moment-of-decision data that would otherwise vanish the second a recruiter closes a tab.

## 3. Top three data sources to ingest first, ranked by ROI per unit of effort

Past shortlists with their advance/reject outcomes come first, by a wide margin. This is the only training signal in the system that lets the Brain learn fit better than the open market does. The candidate profiles attached are commodity; the labels on them are not. If the firm has any ATS, tracker, or even a half-decent spreadsheet history, ingestion is moderate effort and the payoff is enormous. Skip this and everything downstream is unsupervised guessing — you're just building a fancier RAG over public data.

Recruiter mailboxes second. One OAuth connector to Gmail or Outlook reconstructs the relationship graph for free, fully timestamped. Who replied, who went cold, who introduced whom to whom. The effort-to-value ratio is excellent precisely because email is already structured and indexed; you don't have to extract entities from prose, you can read them off the `from:` and `to:` lines.

Interview and client debrief notes third. The raw content here is the highest-value of anything in the firm — this is where rejection reasons get their texture, where "great on paper, weak in the room" lives. But the ingestion effort is genuinely hard: notes are scattered across Notion, Google Docs, voice memos, half-remembered post-interview calls. Third only because the effort is real, not because the value is lower. Once sources one and two are stable, source three is where the next year of improvement comes from.

## 4. What the system should learn over time

The most valuable thing for the Brain to learn is each client's **revealed fit function** — the gap between what they wrote in the brief and who they actually advanced. Humans re-learn this every engagement and forget it between engagements. A client who told us "must be commercial" but then rejected three commercial-leaning candidates and advanced two policy wonks is teaching us something they don't know they're teaching us.

Closely related, **structured rejection reasons** — the *why-not*. The why-not is the most valuable label in the system because it's also the one humans forget fastest. Nobody writes it down at the moment, because at the moment everyone moves on to the next candidate. "Too bank-y." "Strong on paper, weak in the room." "Wanted more front-office posture." These are exactly the texture that trains the next search before it starts.

The Brain should also learn **closed-loop scorer calibration**: which Jatayu sub-scores actually predicted placement, over time. We weighted commercial orientation at 10% on Mandate A based on what the brief implied. Maybe across thirty placements it turns out it should have been 20%, or maybe it shouldn't be a sub-score at all because every client says they want it and none actually rewards it. Humans won't notice that drift; a Brain that watches every shortlist against every outcome will.

And the unglamorous one: **relationship decay**. The firm's network is an asset that silently depreciates. Three months after a warm introduction the relationship cools; nine months in it's effectively gone. A Brain that tracks last-touch per high-value contact and surfaces "due a touch" on the dashboard recovers value that's already sitting on the books.

## 5. The non-obvious insight

The obvious LLM-summary version of this question is *"build a centralized knowledge base over your candidate data, past searches, and recruiter communications, so institutional knowledge is never lost and any recruiter can query it. Capture tacit expert knowledge and use RAG to make it searchable."* That's the answer Claude or GPT produces in 30 seconds if you ask the question cold. Centralize the positives, retrieve them on demand.

My view is that this answer is almost exactly backwards.

The positive data — candidate profiles, briefs, completed mandates — is commodity. Every competitor has it or can buy it tomorrow. The defensible asset is the **negative space**: the rejections, the declines, the flame-outs, labelled per client. A Brain that ingests profiles produces commodity-in-commodity-out. A Brain that ingests *the moments at which candidates were rejected, and why*, produces a kind of judgment a competitor cannot copy even if they have an identical candidate database.

The second half of the insight, which matters even more in practice: the written brief is a lossy proxy that the Brain should learn to distrust. Two clients write near-identical briefs and hire entirely different people. The brief is what the client can articulate. The advance/reject pattern is what they actually want. A Brain that treats the brief as ground truth and indexes everything against it has missed the point. Its primary job is to model the *gap* between stated and revealed preference, and that requires capturing decisions at the moment they happen — which is why this Brain can't be a sidecar bolted onto an existing process. It has to be wired into the workflow itself, the way Jatayu's nine human checkpoints already are. A firm without a workflow tool has no consistent place to capture the labels and is doomed to rebuild the easy version (RAG over docs) because that's what their tooling supports.

So the non-obvious claim, sharply: most teams will build the centralize-and-retrieve version because their data is already shaped that way. The hard version — capture negatives at the moment of decision through workflow instrumentation — is where the moat is, and it's a precondition for the Brain rather than a feature of it. Owning the recruiter's daily tool isn't adjacent to building the Brain. It is the architecture of the Brain.

## 6. What I'm least sure about

The revealed-preference model is data-hungry, and a boutique search firm may not have the placement volume to feed it. A firm doing tens of placements a year, spread across many one-off and repeat clients, has a brutal cold-start problem. Per client you might have a handful of advance/reject decisions — nowhere near enough to fit a per-client preference model that beats the brief.

The honest worry is that my insight is directionally right but operationally premature. The negatives are the moat in principle, but at boutique scale the firm might get more value, sooner, from the boring relationship CRM and feeder-firm graph (sources two and one from question three respectively) than from a learned fit function that's starved of labels.

The de-risking path is to start with **aggregate role-type preferences** — pool labels across similar clients into a single category like "SG sole-officer compliance" and only specialize per client once a specific client has eight or ten data points in the log. Where exactly that threshold lives, and whether a boutique firm ever reaches it within a reasonable time, is the part I genuinely don't know. It's the difference between this being a five-year strategic asset and a two-year build that never quite earns out.
